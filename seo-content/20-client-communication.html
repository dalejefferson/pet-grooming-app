The Perfect Intake Process

Your intake process sets the tone for your entire relationship with a client. Get it right, and you've built a foundation of trust. Get it wrong, and you're fighting uphill battles forever.

Most groomers make intake an afterthought. A few quick questions, maybe a signature on a form, and they're off to the tub.

Big mistake.

The Five-Minute Investment

Spend five minutes on intake, and you'll save hours of headaches down the road. Here's your framework:

  1. Health History Deep Dive — Don't just ask "any health issues?" Probe specifically: seizures, heart conditions, arthritis, skin sensitivities, anxiety triggers. Write everything down.
  2. Grooming History — When was the last groom? Any previous bad experiences? What did they like or dislike about other groomers?
  3. Expectation Alignment — Show photos. Ask them to describe exactly what they want. Repeat it back to confirm.
  4. Policy Review — Don't just hand them a form. Walk through key policies verbally. Matting fees. Late pickup charges. Emergency contacts.
  5. The Permission Question — "If I find something concerning during the groom—a lump, skin issue, or severe matting—how would you like me to handle it?"

Pro Tip: Document Everything

Take photos at check-in. Coat condition. Any visible skin issues. Pre-existing injuries. This protects you from accusations and provides evidence if disputes arise.

The First Impression Formula

Within the first 30 seconds, clients decide if they trust you. Make those seconds count:

  • Greet the pet first (clients love this)
  • Use the pet's name immediately
  • Make eye contact with the owner
  • Project calm confidence, even if you're slammed

Setting Expectations That Stick

Unrealistic expectations are the root of most client conflicts. And here's the uncomfortable truth: most unrealistic expectations are created by groomers.

When you say "I'll do my best" without explaining limitations, you're setting yourself up for disappointment—theirs and yours.

The Honest Conversation Framework

Use this structure for every consultation:

  1. Assess — Examine the coat thoroughly before discussing options.
  2. Educate — Explain what's possible given the current condition.
  3. Options — Present choices with pros and cons of each.
  4. Agreement — Get verbal confirmation of the plan.
  5. Document — Note the agreed-upon service in writing.

Sample Script: Matted Dog

"I've examined Bella's coat and found significant matting, especially behind her ears and on her legs. I have two options for you. Option one: I can attempt to brush this out, but it would take several hours and be uncomfortable for Bella—and honestly, the coat may not recover well. Option two: we do a shorter clip that removes the matting humanely and lets her skin breathe. She'll be comfortable, and the coat will grow back healthy. Which would you prefer?"

Notice what this script does: it presents facts, shows expertise, offers choices, and lets the client decide. No blame. No judgment. Just professional guidance.

Time Expectation Management

Always give time ranges, not exact times. "Between 2 and 3 hours" sets appropriate expectations. "Two hours" creates a client watching the clock at 2:01.

And always pad your estimates. Better to call early and delight than call late and disappoint.

Delivering Bad News Gracefully

Finding a tumor. Discovering severe skin infection. Realizing you can't complete the requested style due to coat damage.

These conversations are never easy. But they're opportunities to build profound trust—if you handle them right.

The CARE Method

Use this framework for any bad news delivery:

C — Calm

Take a breath. Your energy transfers to the client. If you panic, they panic.

A — Acknowledge

Start with acknowledgment: "I found something I need to share with you."

R — Report

State facts simply. No speculation. No medical diagnoses. Just what you observed.

E — Empathize

Connect emotionally: "I know this isn't easy to hear. I'd want to know if it were my pet."

Sample Script: Finding a Lump

"While grooming Max today, I found a small lump on his right side near his hip. It's about the size of a pea. I'm not a vet, so I can't tell you what it is, but I wanted to make you aware so you can have his doctor check it out. It might be nothing, but it's worth getting looked at. Would you like me to show you exactly where it is?"

When You Make a Mistake

Accidents happen. A nicked ear. A too-short cut. A missed matting spot.

What separates professionals from amateurs is what happens next.

The Wrong Approach

Hiding the mistake, making excuses, getting defensive, or blaming the dog. These destroy trust instantly and often lead to negative reviews.

The Right Approach

Immediate transparency. Own it completely. Explain what happened, what you've done to address it, and what you'll do to make it right. Offer appropriate compensation without being asked.

"I made a mistake today" are four of the most powerful trust-building words in any service business.

Handling Complaints Like a Pro

A complaint isn't a crisis. It's an opportunity disguised as a problem.

Research consistently shows that clients who have complaints resolved well are more loyal than clients who never had issues at all.

70% of complaining clients will return if their issue is resolved

The HEAT Technique

  • H — Hear them out. Don't interrupt. Don't defend. Just listen until they've said everything.
  • E — Empathize. "I understand why you're upset. I would be too."
  • A — Apologize. Even if you don't think you were wrong, apologize for their experience. "I'm sorry this wasn't what you expected."
  • T — Take action. Offer a specific solution. Not "what can I do?" but "here's what I'd like to do for you."

The Angry Client Protocol

When someone is truly upset, logic doesn't work. They're operating from emotion. You need to meet them there first.

  1. Lower your voice. Speak softer than they do. It's psychologically impossible to yell at someone whispering.
  2. Move them. "Let's step over here where we can talk privately." Movement breaks emotional patterns.
  3. Validate. "You clearly care deeply about Buster. That's exactly the kind of owner I love working with."
  4. Redirect to solutions. "What would make this right for you?"

Sample Script: Style Complaint

"I hear you—this isn't what you pictured, and that's frustrating. I want to make this right. Can you show me a photo of what you were hoping for? If I can adjust it today, I will at no extra charge. If it needs to grow out first, I'd like to offer your next groom free so we can get it exactly how you want it."

Managing Difficult Personalities

Not every difficult client is being unreasonable. Some are anxious. Some are misinformed. Some have had bad experiences elsewhere.

Understanding the type of difficult you're dealing with changes your approach entirely.

The Anxious Helicopter

They call constantly. They want updates. They worry about everything.

What they need: Information and reassurance.

Your approach: Proactive communication. Send a text when you start. Send a photo mid-groom. Call 15 minutes before pickup. Beat them to the punch, and they'll relax.

The Know-It-All

They've watched YouTube videos. They tell you how to do your job. They question every decision.

What they need: To feel respected and heard.

Your approach: Acknowledge their knowledge. "You've clearly done your research. I love clients who are engaged." Then redirect: "With my experience with this particular breed, I've found that..."

The Price Challenger

They negotiate everything. They compare you to cheaper options. They question your value.

What they need: To understand value, not just cost.

Your approach: Don't defend your prices—explain your value. "My rate includes premium products, my years of specialized training, and the one-on-one attention your dog receives for the entire appointment. The cheaper options often have multiple dogs in the salon at once."

When to Fire a Client

Sometimes the right answer is goodbye. If a client:

  • Repeatedly disrespects you or your staff
  • Makes unreasonable demands after fair solutions are offered
  • Consistently no-shows or cancels last minute
  • Creates a negative environment that affects other clients

...it may be time to professionally part ways. Your peace of mind has value too.

Building Unshakeable Loyalty

Loyal clients are your business's foundation. They don't price-shop. They refer friends. They forgive occasional mistakes.

Loyalty isn't built through discounts. It's built through connection.

The Personal Touch System

  • Remember details. Their dog's quirks. Their vacation plans. Their kids' names. Note these in your software and reference them.
  • Celebrate milestones. Birthday treats for pets. Congratulations on new puppies. Sympathy cards for loss.
  • Create consistency. Same groomer each time when possible. Familiar faces build trust.
  • Surprise occasionally. A free nail trim. A new bandana. Small unexpected gestures create memorable experiences.

The Follow-Up That Matters

After every groom, especially with new clients, send a quick follow-up. A text the next day asking how the pet is doing. A request for feedback. This shows you care beyond the transaction.

Automate Without Losing Heart

Use grooming software to automate reminders and follow-ups, but personalize them. "Buster is due for his trim!" feels better than generic "Your pet needs grooming." Technology should enhance personal touches, not replace them.
