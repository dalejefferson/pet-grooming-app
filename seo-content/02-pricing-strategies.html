The Undercharging Epidemic (And Why It's Killing Your Business)

Let's talk about the elephant in the grooming salon.

You're probably not charging enough.

Not because you don't deserve more. Not because your work isn't excellent. But because somewhere along the way, you started believing that lower prices mean more clients, and more clients mean more money.

Here's the brutal truth: that math doesn't work.

of pet groomers report earning less than they need to cover business expenses and a reasonable salary

Think about it. You spend years developing skills. You invest thousands in equipment, training, and certifications. You handle difficult dogs with patience and skill that most people couldn't imagine.

And then you charge the same as the guy who took a weekend course?

It's time to change that. Let's build a pricing strategy that reflects your true value.

Calculate Your True Costs (Most Groomers Skip This)

Before you can price profitably, you need to know what it actually costs to groom a dog. Not a guess. Not what you think. The real number.

The Full Cost Formula

Your minimum price per groom should be:

(Operating Costs / Monthly Grooms) + Labor Cost + Profit Margin

What to Include in Operating Costs

Expense Category Monthly Cost (Example)
Rent/Mortgage $1,500
Utilities $300
Insurance $200
Supplies (shampoo, blades, etc.) $400
Equipment maintenance $100
Software/Marketing $150
Continuing education $50
Total $2,700

If you complete 100 grooms per month, your overhead cost per groom is $27. That's before you've paid yourself a single penny.

Don't Forget Labor

Your time has value. If a groom takes 2 hours and you want to earn $30/hour, that's $60 in labor costs. Add your $27 overhead and you're at $87 minimum just to break even.

Want a 20% profit margin? Now you're at $104 per groom.

Reality Check

If you're charging $65 for a full groom on a medium dog, you might actually be losing money on every appointment. That's not a pricing strategy. That's a slow path to burnout and closure.

Pricing Psychology That Works

Numbers matter, but psychology matters more. How you present your prices affects how clients perceive your value.

1. Use Charm Pricing (Carefully)

$79 feels significantly cheaper than $80, even though it's only $1 different. This works for entry-level services.

But for premium services? Round numbers signal quality. $150 sounds more premium than $149. It suggests you're confident in your value.

2. Anchor High, Then Offer Options

Always present your most expensive option first. When clients see a $200 spa package before your $85 standard groom, the standard groom feels like a bargain.

This is called price anchoring, and it's incredibly powerful.

3. Never Apologize for Your Prices

When you say "I know it's a lot, but..." you're telling clients they should be concerned about the price. State your prices with confidence. Period. No explanation needed.

4. Frame Value, Not Cost

Instead of: "A full groom costs $95."

Try: "Your full groom includes a luxurious bath with our signature oatmeal shampoo, hand scissoring, ear cleaning, nail trim, and a spritz of pet-safe cologne. That's $95 for the complete pamper experience."

Same price. Completely different perception.

Create Tiered Pricing Packages

One-size-fits-all pricing leaves money on the table. Different clients have different needs and different budgets.

The Power of Three

Offer three service tiers. Most clients will choose the middle option (this is called the Goldilocks Effect).

Tier What's Included Price
Essential Bath, dry, brush, nail trim $55
Signature (Most Popular) Full groom with haircut, bath, dry, brush, nails, ears, teeth brushing $85
Luxury Spa Everything above + deep conditioning, blueberry facial, paw treatment, bandana $125

By making your middle tier the "most popular," you guide clients toward your most profitable option while making them feel like they're making a smart choice.

How to Raise Prices Without Losing Clients

The fear of raising prices keeps many groomers stuck at unprofitable rates for years. But here's what the data shows: most clients don't leave when prices increase reasonably.

The Price Increase Playbook

  1. Give advance notice: 4-6 weeks is standard. Let clients know in person, by email, and with signage in your salon.
  2. Explain (briefly) why: Rising supply costs, investment in better equipment, or increased training are all valid reasons. Keep it short.
  3. Don't over-apologize: State the change confidently. Excessive apologizing makes clients think they should be upset.
  4. Add value first: Before raising prices, add a small perk like a free bandana or upgraded shampoo. The increase feels justified.
  5. Increase strategically: 5-10% annually is digestible. Jumping 25% at once will cause sticker shock.

What to Say

Sample Price Increase Message

"Starting [date], we're adjusting our prices to reflect our continued investment in premium products and ongoing education. Your loyalty means everything to us, and we're committed to providing the exceptional grooming experience you and [pet name] deserve. New pricing will be: [list prices]. Thank you for being part of the Sit Pretty family!"

Will some clients leave? Maybe a few. But here's the secret: the clients who leave over a $10 increase are often the ones causing you the most stress anyway. You'll replace them with clients who value quality over rock-bottom prices.

Track Profitability Per Service

Not all grooms are created equal. A 2-hour doodle groom might be less profitable than a 45-minute lab bath, even at a higher price point.

What You Should Be Tracking

  • Time spent per service type and breed
  • Product usage per service
  • Revenue per hour of labor
  • Which add-ons are most popular (and profitable)
  • Client retention rates by service tier

This data tells you where to focus. Maybe you should raise prices on doodles. Maybe your bath-only service is actually your most profitable offering.
